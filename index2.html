The breadwinner who waited two years.

“I cannot stop working — my family depends on me. I didn’t want to spend money on myself.”
In-group stereotype

What a “real provider” does — and doesn’t do.

Availability bias

“Hospitals always cost money” — based on hearsay, not truth.

Self-stereotype

“Men endure; they don’t seek help.” A bias he didn’t know he was carrying.

Why he didn’t come for two years.

Lens 01 — Maslow’s Hierarchy

Physiological needs outrank safety.

When food and income aren’t guaranteed, untreated illness becomes the price of survival.

Lens 02 — Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic

Family duty drowned out self-care.

The pull to provide overpowered the pull to heal. The provider role became a cage.

Lens 03 — Self-Determination

Autonomy, competence, relatedness — all denied.

He couldn’t take time off. He didn’t understand the disease. The system felt foreign.

What this experience opened up.

Key insight. Before this experience, healthcare inequality was an abstract concept. Seeing it firsthand revealed the emotional and human dimensions that no textbook fully captures.

The Johari Window taught us that expanding our blind spots is the foundation of authentic, human-centred leadership.

Same words. Different outcome.

01 — Sender

The clinical staff

Rushed, technical, low affect — competent, but emotionally guarded.

02 — Channel

The clinic itself

Crowded room. No privacy. Time pressure. Fear. Low health-literacy.

03 — Receiver

The patient

Anxious, culturally guarded, hearing about their body for the first time.

Almost every patient we tested had high BP or sugar — and almost none of them knew. In healthcare, delivery is the message.

Three lenses. One clinic.

Utilitarian Greatest good for the greatest number — free care, mass screening, real impact at scale. Strong.
Rights-based Each person’s dignity & autonomy. Treated, yes — but not always heard, rarely understood. Mixed.
Virtue Character — empathy, compassion, patience. Not unkind, but emotionally guarded. Weak.

The clinic is utilitarian-strong, virtue-weak. Optimize purely for numbers, and you can erode the very humanity that made the mission worth pursuing.

The competencies this experience grew in us.

Competency 01 — Self-awareness

“Leadership begins with action.”

Leadership begins with awareness — of patients’ blind spots, and our own.

We walked in assuming the problem was clinical. The Johari Window forced us to see how much we were missing — about patients, about the system, and about ourselves as future leaders.

Competency 02 — Reading bias

“Bias is loud and visible.”

Bias is often a story whispered for two years.

The breadwinner who waited two years didn’t shout his bias — he lived it quietly, through in-group stereotypes and availability heuristics. Real leadership listens for the silences.

Competency 03 — Empathy as skill

“Empathy is a personality trait.”

Empathy is a clinical skill — measurable, trainable, deployable.

We watched competent staff lose patients emotionally because empathy wasn’t treated as part of the job. Treating it as a trainable competency is what separates a service from a system that heals.

The SMART goal we are setting.

Long-term goal · 01

Improve patient guidance for first-time visitors.

3months

timeline to put a clear patient guidance system in place.

Specific: redesign the patient intake flow at Skyline. Measurable: tracked through patient feedback and consultation time. Achievable: built on the staff and tools already in the clinic. Relevant: directly addresses the confusion we saw on the ground. Time-bound: in place within 3 months.

How we’ll know it’s working.

KPI 01 — Clarity

Reduce patient confusion.

−40%

fewer repeated questions during intake within 3 months.

Instrument: 3-question post-visit survey in Urdu + staff intake log. Baseline: measured in the first two weeks. Owner: intake lead. Why it matters: confusion was the single biggest barrier we observed — patients couldn’t advocate for what they didn’t understand.

KPI 02 — Flow

Faster consultation flow.

≤ 20min

average check-in to consultation start, without cutting empathy.

Instrument: timestamped intake sheet. Guardrail: if speed goes up but satisfaction goes down, the KPI fails. Review cadence: weekly for 6 weeks, then monthly. Efficiency is only a win if dignity holds.

KPI 03 — Felt experience

Higher patient satisfaction.

≥ 85%

of patients say they “felt heard and understood.”

Instrument: single-question exit card, Urdu-first. Threshold: below 70% triggers a communication workshop. Why it matters: this is the virtue-ethics gap the clinic showed us — strong outcomes, weak felt experience.

The qualities we want to build.

NGH 01

Empathy

Treat each patient as a person first. Listen before prescribing.

What we saw: staff were technically competent but emotionally guarded — the breadwinner left without knowing how his diabetes attacked his eyes.

What it looks like: sit, make eye contact, ask one open question, repeat the patient’s words back in Urdu before diagnosing.

NGH 02

Responsibility

Own the full visit, not just the diagnosis.

What we saw: 9 of 9 patients had elevated BP or sugar; almost none knew. The information existed — no one had taken responsibility for delivering it.

What it looks like: every patient leaves with a one-page Urdu summary of findings, next steps, and red-flag symptoms — signed by the consulting doctor.

NGH 03

Awareness

Notice the blind spots — patients’ and our own.

What we saw: the patient with 60% vision loss had no idea anything was wrong. We ourselves underestimated the emotional weight patients carried.

What it looks like: a monthly Johari debrief — staff name one blind spot they noticed in a patient, and one they noticed in themselves.

NGH 04

Ethical thinking

Hold the clinic to more than efficiency.

What we saw: utilitarian-strong, virtue-weak — the clinic was treating many, but not always honouring each.

What it looks like: when speed and dignity conflict, dignity wins. Reviewed quarterly through the three ethical lenses: utility, rights, virtue.

Awareness is where leadership begins.

We went into Skyline expecting to help patients. We came out aware that the system itself needed help — and that anyone, including us, can choose to lead from where we stand.
