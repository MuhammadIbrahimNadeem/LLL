SLIDE 2 — The Issue (Member 1 — Gamma)
Time: ~1.5 minutes | Owner: Gamma
Title: The Real Issue Isn't Medical — It's Perceptual
Content:

Our hypothesis going in: "People skip eye care because of stigma."
What we actually found: A three-layer perception breakdown

Patients can't see what's wrong with them (literal blind spots about their own health)
Patients misread the system (assume "free = low quality" or "free = costly")
The clinic itself misreads the patient (treats them as a case, not a person)


One issue, three faces — all perceptual.

Speaker Notes:

"Before our fieldwork, we assumed the issue at a charity clinic would be obvious — stigma, money, access. But after nine patients, we realized the deeper issue is that perception itself is the system that's failing. Patients couldn't perceive what was wrong with their own bodies. They misperceived what 'free treatment' meant. And the staff, under pressure, often failed to perceive the patient as a frightened human being. The medicine works. The perception layer doesn't. That's our issue."

Why this passes the "non-obvious" test: A descriptive group would say "patients don't come because of stigma." You're identifying that perception failure happens on both the patient AND the institution side.

SLIDE 3 — Bias, Stereotypes & The Patient's Self-Perception (Member 2)
Time: ~1.5 minutes | Owner: Member 2 (consultation lead)
Title: The Breadwinner Who Waited Two Years
Content:

The Story: Male patient, sole breadwinner, delayed treatment for 2 years
His reasoning:

"I cannot afford to stop working." → economic bias
"Hospitals cost money." → assumption (the clinic is free)
"A man provides; he doesn't get sick." → cultural gender stereotype


Three biases stacked into one decision:

In-group stereotype (what a "real provider" does)
Availability bias (hospital = expensive, based on what he'd heard)
Self-stereotype (men endure; they don't seek help)



Speaker Notes (Member 2 says):

"One patient stayed with me. He was the sole breadwinner of his family. He had delayed eye treatment for two full years — and only came in when his condition got bad enough that he couldn't ignore it. When I asked him why he waited, his answer wasn't 'I was afraid of doctors.' His answer was: 'I cannot stop working — my family depends on me, and I didn't want to spend money.' He had no idea Skyline Trust was free.
What our course taught us is that bias isn't always about other people — it's often a story we tell about ourselves. He had internalized the stereotype that a male provider doesn't take time off for his own health. Combined with the assumption that all hospitals cost money, this man almost lost his sight to a bias he didn't know he was carrying. That's the silent cost of stereotypes in our culture."


SLIDE 4 — Self-Awareness: Johari Window in Action (Member 1 — Gamma)
Time: ~1.5 minutes | Owner: Gamma (since you saw the abnormality firsthand)
Title: The Patient Who Couldn't See Himself
Visual on slide: A 2×2 Johari Window grid
Known to SelfUnknown to SelfKnown to OthersOpenBLIND ← the patient lived hereUnknown to OthersHiddenUnknown
Content:

The Story: A patient walked in for a routine check. Refraction revealed he had lost 60% of vision in one eye. He had no idea.
His reaction: Surprise — "How is this linked to my sugar?"
Johari Analysis:

His "Blind Quadrant" was massive — others (his body, his disease) knew. He didn't.
The clinic's role isn't just to treat — it's to shrink the blind quadrant by giving him feedback he can't get on his own.


Most patients with diabetes or high BP we screened lived in this same blind zone.

Speaker Notes (Gamma says):

"During refraction on one patient, we discovered he had lost 60% of his vision in one eye. He had no clue. His first reaction wasn't anger or grief — it was surprise. He asked me, 'How is this linked to my sugar?' He didn't know his diabetes was destroying his eyes.
This is exactly what the Johari Window calls the Blind Quadrant — the part of yourself that everyone except you can see. His body knew. His disease knew. His doctor would have known if he'd come earlier. He was the only person in the dark.
And here's the leadership insight: the job of a good clinic — and a good leader — isn't only to fix problems. It's to shrink the blind quadrant by giving honest, timely feedback. Most of the patients we screened that day were living in their blind zone. They had high sugar, high BP, deteriorating vision — and no awareness. The clinic's most powerful intervention wasn't the medicine. It was the moment of feedback."


SLIDE 5 — Communication Analysis (Member 3)
Time: ~1.5 minutes | Owner: Member 3 (BP/sugar/drops)
Title: When Information Landed — and When It Didn't
Content:

What worked: When patients heard "your sugar is high — and that's why your eyes are getting worse" with empathy, they listened.
What didn't: When staff delivered the same information flatly, patients nodded but didn't internalize it.
The communication breakdown:

Sender (staff): rushed, low affect, technical
Receiver (patient): anxious, low health-literacy, culturally guarded
Channel noise: crowded clinic, no privacy, time pressure


Lesson: In healthcare, delivery is the message.

Speaker Notes (Member 3 says):

"My role was checking blood pressure and sugar levels and dispensing eye drops. Almost every patient we tested had either high BP or high sugar — and almost none of them knew, because they hadn't been checking regularly. When I told them, the most common reaction was: 'I felt overwhelmed, but I didn't know.'
Here's what I learned about communication: the same information landed completely differently depending on how it was delivered. When I sat down, made eye contact, and explained, 'Your sugar is high, and that's the reason your vision is getting worse — but you can control it,' patients leaned in. When the same information was delivered by rushed staff in three flat sentences, patients nodded politely and walked out. Same words. Different outcome.
Our course teaches the sender-receiver-channel model. In a hospital, the channel is full of noise — fear, embarrassment, low health literacy. A good communicator doesn't just transmit. They adjust until the message lands. In healthcare, delivery is the message."


SLIDE 6 — Ethical Analysis: Three Lenses on One Clinic (Member 2)
Time: ~1.5 minutes | Owner: Member 2
Title: Three Ethical Lenses, One Clinic
Content (table format on slide):

The clinic is utilitarian-strong but virtue-weak.
Treating 50 patients quickly (utilitarian win) can erode the dignity of each one (rights loss) and harden the staff (virtue loss).

Speaker Notes (Member 2 says):

"Our course teaches three ethical frameworks, and we used all three to analyze Skyline.
Through a utilitarian lens — greatest good for the greatest number — Skyline is doing remarkable work. Free treatment, high patient throughput, real impact at scale. By this metric, they pass.
Through a rights-based lens — focused on each person's dignity and autonomy — the picture gets mixed. Patients were treated, yes. But were they truly heard? Did they leave understanding their own condition? Sometimes yes, often no.
And through a virtue lens — which asks about character, empathy, compassion — we observed gaps. The staff weren't unkind, but they were emotionally guarded. Patients walk in already tense; meeting them with cold efficiency, even unintentionally, hurts the healing.
Our ethical insight: the clinic is utilitarian-strong but virtue-weak. And here's the trap — when you optimize purely for numbers, you sometimes lose the very humanity that made the mission worth pursuing in the first place. Real ethics requires balancing all three."


SLIDE 7 — Leadership & Growth: What a Better Leader Would Do (Member 3)
Time: ~2 minutes | Owner: Member 3 (closing speaker)
Title: From Observation to Leadership: What We Would Change
Content:
The Leadership Gap We Saw:

"Hospital staff were technically competent but emotionally distant. As a patient, you walk in tense — and meet someone who isn't listening."

What an Effective Leader Would Do (using NGH + SMART + KPI):

Our Personal Growth:

Gamma — learned that leadership begins with awareness: of patients' blind spots and our own.
Member 2 — learned that bias isn't always loud — sometimes it's a quiet story someone tells themselves for two years.
Member 3 — learned that empathy is a clinical skill, not a personality trait.

Speaker Notes (Member 3 closes):

"So what would we do differently as leaders? Three things.
First, using the Next Goal Higher framework — we'd raise the bar from 'treat the patient' to 'make the patient feel seen.' That's a SMART goal: three empathy-and-communication training workshops in 90 days, measured by a simple KPI — the percentage of patients who report they felt heard. Target: 85% or above.
Second, every patient leaves with a one-page summary in Urdu explaining their condition. KPI: 100% delivery. This shrinks the blind quadrant we talked about earlier.
Third, BP and sugar screening becomes mandatory at every eye visit, no exceptions. Most of the eye damage we saw was downstream of uncontrolled diabetes.
But the deepest lesson isn't operational — it's personal. I learned that empathy is a clinical skill, not a personality trait. You can train it. You can measure it. And in a healthcare setting, it isn't a soft add-on — it's the difference between a patient who returns and one who quietly disappears.
The professor told us: 'awareness is where leadership begins.' We went into Skyline expecting to help patients. We came out aware that the system itself needed help — and that anyone, including us, can choose to lead from wherever we stand. Thank you."
